Who am I?

Growing up in Somerset I had the chance to visit many sites along the Jurassic Coast. During this time I developed a keen interest in fossil hunting, despite never finding much of value. Seeing these fossilised animals and wondering how they lived stuck with me. Paired with my Grandpa's rock collection and travel tapes, this led to a fascination with how things worked all around the world and a desire to understand science.

Current interests

My current main interest is in tectonic hazards (Volcanoes, earthquakes, tsunamis). These natural hazards have always been around and wont just disappear. To understand their mechanisms and results is key in mitigating damage and potential harm to life. Seeing such events take place throughout my life, and hearing about those before, surely strengthened my desire to study Geology.

Out of all the hazards, I am most interested in volcanics because, at the end of the day, seeing them erupt is just pretty cool. Being able to glimpse deep-rooted processes occuring all across the world is simply incredible. Travelling around has allowed me to see some of these for myself, be it the eggy smelling Solfatara in Italy, or the base of my masters project on the island of Tenerife.

Outside of geology I have a long-lasting interest in computers and programming. Starting around the age of 9 to automate tasks on a computer to creating small games. Computers have aided in widening my world-view as well as providing opportunities to learn about things I would have never thought of. In this regard computers have been especially beneficial in learning foreign languages through a wealth of online sources.

Economic Geology

Secretary for the University of Southampton chapter of the Society of Economic Geology
2019-2020

During my 3rd year studying at the University of Southampton I was appointed the secretary for the Society of Economic Geology.

Interest grew during SOES3002 ('Petroleum Geology and Mineral Resources'), especially in mineral exploration at arc settings. Seeing a link between volcanic systems and profitable excursions ignited a passion for exploration geology.
My main interest was in the formation of porphyry copper deposits at subduction margins.

Team Environment

The whole is greater than the sum of its parts.
Aristotle

Studying at Southampton has allowed for many teamwork opportunities. From mapping trips to group projects a strong sense of teamwork was required to complete the tasks.

Working both independently within the group as well as providing each other with help and feedback facilitated efficient progress on projects.

During my 3rd year mapping project I worked alongside my mapping partner to ensure safety as well as bouncing ideas off of each other. This was further continued in 4th year mapping of Cornish localities within larger groups. Here the focus was on creating reports and delegating roles. Within this project my group achieved a 1st.

Another 4th year project involved working as a team to conduct an offshore site investigation for a proposed windfarm. Roles were delegated based on specialties as the group was made up of students from differing disciplines. Working together and explaining our different areas to each other allowed us to achieve a high 2:1.
