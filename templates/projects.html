3rd Year Dissertation

A seamless onshore-offshore map of the Osmington Mills and Ringstead Bay area

The mapping region spans from Bowleaze Cove, east Weymouth, to White Nothe, just west of Lulworth Cove. It is found within the Wessex basin, comprising of Jurassic clays through to upper Cretaceous chalks.

Field Methods

3 weeks of onsite mapping were carried out in April 2020.

Field sheets were generated from OS data using ArcGIS at the scale of 1:5000.

The ‘green line’ method was followed for in-field analysis. This entailed following the coastline and picking out localities based on exposure. At these exposures a description was recorded within the field notebook and any possible measurements were taken (strike/dip, paleocurrent, lineation’s etc).

Outcrop was bubbled onto the sheets allowing for boundary interpretations before digitising in ArcGIS.

Digital Methods

Digital reconnaissance was carried out by sourcing and importing Lidar layers to GIS software (ArcGIS in this case) before manipulating them to provide quantitative data.

This project calls on data from DORIS (Dorset integrated seabed study) for the bathymetry, and then Digimap data as well as CCO (Channel coastal observatory) for online data sets.

Quantitative data was extracted by producing slope and aspect layers and storing data in Excel documents. Aspect was used for strike analysis whilst slope gave dip analysis.

Aerial photography aided with hypotheses creation with continuity of features. Doris bathymetry revealed weathering patterns, helping to plot lithological boundaries offshore.

Slope stability was analysed using maths functions between differing Lidar layers. This was analysed alongside lithology to study the nature of failure and any potential causes.

Data sources:

Digimap Lidar: 2006,2007,2008,2009
Digimap Aerial Photography: 2014,2017
CCO Lidar: 2007,2009,2014,2018
CCO Aerial Photography: 2013,2019
DORIS Bathymetry: integrated 2008/09

Masters Project

Geochemical observations of an evolving magma system beneath Tenerife

Tenerife is a central member of the Canary Island chain off the north-western coast of Africa. The oldest exposed units here are the
"Old Basaltic series" dated at 12 Ma. The island has undergone both mafic and felsic eruptions with both types observed directly ontop
of each other. This project focused on a 110m section of the Las Cañadas caldera wall to try and understand what is occuring beneath
Tenerife.

Lab Preparation

32 rock samples were collected from the Las Cañadas caldera wall.
These were then crushed with a non-torque press, sieved to remove grains larger than 1mm and less than 0.5mm before being cleaned in an ultrasonic bath.
Around 0.3g of sample was picked under microscope to remove alteration material and any contaminants.
Further samples were crushed in an agate pestle and mortar for powder analysis.
Mother and daughter solutions were produced for ICP-MS analysis.

The results showed that the Canary plume is a HiMu source, fed from the lower mantle. The plume is evidenced as stalling at the upper-most mantle.
Based on published data ages were assigned to this section of the Las Cañadas caldera wall.
The formation has a published age of 0.37Ma-0.175Ma, however the data suggests that the base may be older.
Mafic samples appear to reflect rapid ascent of magma in quench conditions with increased Ti values. Felsic samples are more evolved, with large Eu anomalies and enriched LREE.
Tenerife appears to have a periodically refilled magma chamber.
